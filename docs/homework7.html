Stochastic Differential Equations

A stochastic differential equation (SDE) is a differential equation in which one or more of the terms is a stochastic process. In particular, they are differential equations that involve deterministic and random components. These are used to model the evolution where randomess plays a significant role. Stochastic differential equations provide a link between probability theory and the much older and more developed fields of ordinary and partial differential equations. Some of the most important are:
  1. Geometric Brownian Motion
    An Geometric Brownian Motion (also known as the exponential Brownian Motion) is a continuous-time stochastic process. In particular, it's used in mathematical finance to model stock prices in a particular financial model. This process is best described by its SDE, which is the following:
    dS_{t} = \mu \ S_t \ dt + \sigma \ S_t \ dW_{t}
    This ultimately expresses the changes of the Brownian; in particular the process changes by a costant amount every infinitesimal period of time. Formally, the first constant is called drift, while the second is called diffusion term. In particular:
    • S_t rapresents the stock prices in a particular time t,
    • \mu is the drift, which is the average rate of return per time unit,
    • \sigma is the volatility, which rapresents the standard deviation of the flactuations,
    • dW_t is a Wiener process (or the Brownian motion), which rapresents the random and continuous movements.

    • X_t rapresents the process value at time t,

    • \sigma is the volatility,

  3. Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Process
    An Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Process (also known as the Mean Reverting Process) is a stochastic proces with applications in financial mathematics and physical sciences. Its original application was as a model for the velocity of the Brownian particle under a particular friction. Its SDE is the following:
    dX_{t} = \theta (\mu - X_t) dt + \sigma \ dW_{t}
    The constant parameters are:
    • \theta which is the rate (or speed) of the mean reversion. It is defined as strictly bigger than 0,
    • \mu is the long term mean of the process. In particular, the process will tend to this particular mean,
    • \sigma is the volatility.
    By ignoring the random flactuations (given by the Weiner process), we see that X_t has an overall drift towards the mean value, as expressed above. The process reverts to this mean exponentially, with rate \theta . An example is shown on the image on the right:
    N = 80; \ M = 250; \ P = 0.5; \ \sigma = 0.4; \ \mu = 12; \ \theta = 0.2

    A Vasicek Model is a model that fundamentally follows the same same SDE of the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Process, however the parameter \theta can be a non-constant, in particular it can be a deterministic function of t (1).

    A Hull-White Model is an extension of the Vasicek model with the difference that it can be made to fit the current term structure (2). In particular, it's SDE is the following:

    dX_t = [\theta (t) - \mu X_t] dt + \sigma \ dW_t

  4. Cox-Ingersoll-Ross (CIR) Model
    A Cox-Ingersoll-Ross (CIR) Model is similar to the Vasicek Model; however it includes a term proportional to the square root of the process. Its SDE is the following:
    dX_t = \kappa \ ( \ \theta - X_t \ ) dt + \sigma \ \sqrt{X_t} \ dW_t
  5. Black-Karasinski Model
    A Black-Karasinski Model can be considered as an extension of the CIR Model. It introduces a random (or stochastic) volatility, allowing this parameter to vary over time. It's SDE is the following:
    dX_t = [\ \theta(t) - \alpha X_t \ ] \ dt + \sigma(t) \ \sqrt{X_t} \ dW_t
    Where :
    • \alpha is the speed of the mean reversion,
    • \theta (t) is the mean reversion function, with variable t,
    • \sigma(t) is the volatility function, with variable t,
    • X_t is the short rate at time t.
  6. Heston Model
    A Heston Model is a mathematical model that describes the evolution of the volatility of an underlying asset. This is defined by two stochastic equations, one for the price and one for the volatility. It's SDE are:
    dS_t = \mu \ S_t \ dt + \sigma \ \sqrt{V_t} \ S_t \ dW^1_t
    dV_t = \kappa \ ( \ \theta - V_t \ ) dt + \sigma \ \sqrt{V_t} \ dW^2_t
    Where :
    • S_t is the asset price at time t,
    • \sigma is the volatility,
    • V_t is the instantaneous variance at time t, which is the volatility squared,
    • \kappa is the speed of mean reversion of volatility,
    • \mu is the average rate of return,
    • dW^1_t and dW^2_t are correlated to the Weiner process.
  7. Chen Model
    A Chen Model is a more complex stochastic volatility model that considers both jumps and continuous-time processes in the volatility equation. It's SDE is:
    dX_t = ( \mu - \lambda X_t ) \ dt + \sigma \sqrt{X_t} dW_t + dJ_t
    Where :
    • X_t is the volatility of the asset at a particular moment in time,
    • \mu is the average growth rate,
    • \lambda is the mean reverting speed,
    • \sigma is the volatility of the process,
    • dJ_t is a jump process.
