Gambler's Ruin Problem


Gambler's Ruin Problem is a classic problem in probability theory. In this famous scenario, a gambler starts with an initial amount of \( i \) dollars. On each game, the gambler wins 1$ with probability \( p \) and loses 1$ with probability \( q = p - 1 \). We have to keep in mind that both p and q are probability numbers, therefore must be defined as bigger or equals than 0 and smaller or equals than 1. The gambler will stop playing once he has lost all his money, or has accumulated \( N \) dollars with his wins. This problem's question aims to find the probability that the gambler will end up with N dollars.
Let's being with a fair game, where p is one half. Considering \( X(t) \) to be the amount of dollars the gamblers has, we expect the dollars on the next round to be: $$ E[X(t+1)] = \frac{1}{2}(E[X(t)] - 1) + \frac{1}{2}(E[X(t)] + 1) $$ Hence when it is a fair game, the probability that the gambler walks out of the casino with \(N\) dollars is: \( \frac{i}{N} \). In reality, the games in casino are usually unfair.
