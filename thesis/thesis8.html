Stochastic processes and SDE's

In probability theory and related fields, a stochastic or random process is a mathematical object, usually defined as a sequence of random variables, where the index is usually an interpretation for time. Stochastic processes are widely used as mathematical models of systems and phenomena that appear to behave randomly. Stochastic processes can be either discrete or continuous regarding to time. In discrete-time , the random variable evolves at distinct and particular points in time, while the continuous-time evolves continuously. Stochastic processes hold several properties, such as:

  1. A stochastic process is said stationary if its statistical properties remain constant over time, such as the mean, variance and correlation structure.
  2. A stochastic process has the Markov property if the future behavior of the process only depends on its current state, and not on its past history.
  3. A stochastic process is a martingale if its expected value at any future time, given its current value, is equal to its current value.
  4. A stochastic process is said to be ergodic if the time average of a random variable converges to the expected value as the time goes to infinity.

A stochastic differential equation (SDE) is a differential equation in which one or more of the terms is a stochastic process. In particular, they are differential equations that involve deterministic and random components. These are used to model the evolution where randomess plays a significant role. Stochastic differential equations provide a link between probability theory and the much older and more developed fields of ordinary and partial differential equations. The general form of a one-dimensional SDE is the following: $$ dX_t = a(t, X_t) dt + b(t, X_t) dW_t $$ In this model,

  1. \( X_t \) is the process,
  2. \( a(t, X_t) \) and \( b(t, X_t) \) are deterministc functions,
  3. \( dW_t \) rapresents the stochastic part, or Brownian motion.

In general, SDEs provide a way to describe the evolution of a system over time, being influenced by deterministc functions as well as random fluctuations. A stochastic process is the solution for an SDE. However, this is usually simulated, since finding a precise and mathematical solution is a very complicated task that requires a lot of time and complex calculations. Some of the most important SDEs are:

  1. Geometric Brownian Motion

    An Geometric Brownian Motion (also known as the exponential Brownian Motion) is a continuous-time stochastic process. In particular, it's used in mathematical finance to model stock prices in a particular financial model. This process is best described by its SDE, which is the following: $$ dS_{t} = \mu \ S_t \ dt + \sigma \ S_t \ dW_{t} $$ This ultimately expresses the changes of the Brownian; in particular the process changes by a costant amount every infinitesimal period of time. Formally, the first constant is called drift, while the second is called diffusion term. In particular:

    • \( S_t \) rapresents the stock prices in a particular time t,
    • \(\mu \) is the drift, which is the average rate of return per time unit,
    • \( \sigma \) is the volatility, which rapresents the standard deviation of the flactuations,
    • \( dW_t \) is a Wiener process (or the Brownian motion), which rapresents the random and continuous movements.

  3. Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Process

    An Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Process (also known as the Mean Reverting Process) is a stochastic proces with applications in financial mathematics and physical sciences. Its original application was as a model for the velocity of the Brownian particle under a particular friction. Its SDE is the following: $$ dX_{t} = \theta (\mu - X_t) dt + \sigma \ dW_{t} $$ The constant parameters are:

    • \( \theta \) which is the rate (or speed) of the mean reversion. It is defined as strictly bigger than 0,
    • \( \mu \) is the long term mean of the process. In particular, the process will tend to this particular mean,
    • \( \sigma \) is the volatility.

    By ignoring the random flactuations (given by the Weiner process), we see that \( X_t \) has an overall drift towards the mean value, as expressed above. The process reverts to this mean exponentially, with rate \( \theta \). An example is shown on the image on the right:

    $$ N = 80; \ M = 250; \ P = 0.5; \ \sigma = 0.4; \ \mu = 12; \ \theta = 0.2 $$

    A Vasicek Model is a model that fundamentally follows the same same SDE of the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Process, however the parameter \( \theta \) can be a non-constant, in particular it can be a deterministic function of \( t \) (7).

    A Hull-White Model is an extension of the Vasicek model with the difference that it can be made to fit the current term structure (9). In particular, it's SDE is the following: $$ dX_t = [\theta (t) - \mu X_t] dt + \sigma \ dW_t $$

  4. Cox-Ingersoll-Ross (CIR) Model
    A Cox-Ingersoll-Ross (CIR) Model is similar to the Vasicek Model; however it includes a term proportional to the square root of the process. Its SDE is the following: $$ dX_t = \kappa \ ( \ \theta - X_t \ ) dt + \sigma \ \sqrt{X_t} \ dW_t $$
  5. Black-Karasinski Model
    A Black-Karasinski Model can be considered as an extension of the CIR Model. It introduces a random (or stochastic) volatility, allowing this parameter to vary over time. It's SDE is the following: $$ dX_t = [\ \theta(t) - \alpha X_t \ ] \ dt + \sigma(t) \ \sqrt{X_t} \ dW_t $$ Where :
    • \( \alpha \) is the speed of the mean reversion,
    • \( \theta (t) \) is the mean reversion function, with variable t,
    • \( \sigma(t) \) is the volatility function, with variable t,
    • \( X_t \) is the short rate at time t.
  6. Heston Model
    A Heston Model is a mathematical model that describes the evolution of the volatility of an underlying asset. This is defined by two stochastic equations, one for the price and one for the volatility. It's SDE are: $$ dS_t = \mu \ S_t \ dt + \sigma \ \sqrt{V_t} \ S_t \ dW^1_t $$ $$ dV_t = \kappa \ ( \ \theta - V_t \ ) dt + \sigma \ \sqrt{V_t} \ dW^2_t $$ Where :
    • \( S_t \) is the asset price at time t,
    • \( \sigma \) is the volatility,
    • \( V_t \) is the instantaneous variance at time t, which is the volatility squared,
    • \( \kappa \) is the speed of mean reversion of volatility,
    • \( \mu \) is the average rate of return,
    • \( dW^1_t \) and \( dW^2_t \) are correlated to the Weiner process.
  7. Chen Model
    A Chen Model is a more complex stochastic volatility model that considers both jumps and continuous-time processes in the volatility equation. It's SDE is: $$ dX_t = ( \mu - \lambda X_t ) \ dt + \sigma \sqrt{X_t} dW_t + dJ_t $$ Where :
    • \( X_t \) is the volatility of the asset at a particular moment in time,
    • \( \mu \) is the average growth rate,
    • \( \lambda \) is the mean reverting speed,
    • \( \sigma \) is the volatility of the process,
    • \( dJ_t \) is a jump process.
