The Gaussian distribution, meaning, derivations and simulations

The Gaussian distribution (or normal distribution ) is a type of continuous probability distribution for a real-valued random variable. The density function of the variable is the following: $$ f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2 \pi}} e^{-\frac{1}{2} ( \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma} )} $$ The parameter \( \mu \) is the mean of the distribution. In this particular distribution, mean, median and mode coincide. \( \sigma \) on the other hand, is the standard deviation, while the variance of this distribution is \( \sigma^2 \). The Gaussian function is said to be "normally distributed". This distribution is used across all science field, given its importance to represent unknown real-valued ramdom variable and the CTL, discussed in the previous thesis.

Standard normal distribution

The simplest case of a normal distribution is known as the standard normal distribution. This is a special case where \( \mu = 0 \) and \( \sigma = 1 \), and it's described by the following probability density function: $$ \varphi(z) = \frac{e^{-z^2/2}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}$$

General normal distribution

Every normal distribution is a version of the standard normal distribution. We can descrive those classes of functions thanks to the following: $$ f(x | \mu, \sigma^2) = \frac{1}{\sigma} \varphi (\frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}) $$ The domain here has been stretched by a factor of \( \sigma \) and has been translated by \( \mu \).
