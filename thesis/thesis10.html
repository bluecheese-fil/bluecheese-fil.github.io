The Wiener process and the GBM, Derivations and simulations

The Wiener process, also known as Brownian motion, is a continuous-time stochastic process that describes the random motion of particles supended in a medium. A Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM), also known as exponential Brownian motion is a continuous-time process where the logarithm of the randomly varying quantity follows a Brownian motion. This process is particularly useful in mathematical finance to model stock prices. The Wiener process obeys the following stochastic differential equation (SDE): $$ dW_t = \mu S_t dt + \sigma dZ_t $$ Where \( Z_t \) is the standard Brownian motion, \( \mu \) and \( \sigma \) are constants, in particular the drift and volatility. On the other hand, the geometric brownian motion follows a different SDE: $$ dS_t = \mu S_t dt + \sigma S_t dZ_t $$ Where \( W_t \) is the Wiener process (or Brownian motion), \( \mu \) rapresents the drift and \( \sigma \) is the percentage volatility. These processes have various applications in multiple fields, such as finance, physics, computer science and biology. Some of the most important applications are:
  • Biology: Population dynamics,
  • Computer Science: Monte Carlo simulations,
  • Engineering: Signal processing,
  • Finance: Option pricing, Risk management and Stock price modeling,
  • Climate modeling.
